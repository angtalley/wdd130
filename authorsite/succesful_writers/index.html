Successful Writers

Stephen King. Rick Riordan. Shannon Messenger. What do these names have in common? They are all modern writers. Every writer has a writer’s style and voice. They explore challenges of the human heart and world through words. Have you ever wondered how these writers became so popular? Why their writing is so intriguing and relevant? The Brave New World author Aldous Huxley said, “Word can be like X-rays if you use them properly—they’ll go through anything. You read and you’re pierced.” We explore the techniques of writers who have crafted words that became X-rays, and examine why those techniques are so powerful. We also discuss how to replicate a technique. Explore this page and leave a comment for an author you would love to learn more about.

A Critique on Technique: Eoin Colfer's Artemis Fowl

I remember running out of my bedroom with a beat-up slightly torn copy of Eragon. I asked my mom, “what next?” and she walked me to the massive bookshelf that spanned the entire living room wall. That is when I was introduced to Eoin Colfer’s Artemis Fowl. I was entranced.

The first book in the series achieves something not many books do—it is written as if it were a camera lens focusing on the main character, Artemis Fowl. The first sentence, “Ho Chi Minh City in the summer,” immediately places the reader on this zoom lens, where Colfer takes the reader through the city, getting more and more narrow, until it lands on Artemis with the simple sentence, “sun did not suit Artemis.” I adore the zoom/lens technique because a well-executed setup reads just how a movie would unfold and can focus on something small like Artemis’s two left feet, or something as grand and unfamiliar as Haven City, an underground fantasy fairy city.

The biggest technique Colfer has mastered is the use of simple to complex language and plot that shift throughout the novels. The first book targets children around 10-12 years old, however, the final book targets late teens/young adults. Colfer wrote a series that matures with its audience. At the climax of the first novel, the big reveal is that Holly, an elf LEPrecon (police officer), escaped Artemis’s cell.
